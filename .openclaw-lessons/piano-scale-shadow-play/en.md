---
name: Scale Shadow Play
description: Make piano scales more musical by letting one hand lead and the other shadow with delayed control, tone, and timing.
---
This lesson turns scale practice into a stage scene. Choose **C major / A minor** or **D major / B minor** and set **72-88 BPM**. One hand is the actor, the other is the shadow. The shadow must stay close enough to match, but different enough to teach control.
<!-- block -->
Start with the **actor round**. Play one octave up and down with the right hand alone, then the left hand alone. Listen for three things:
- even tone
- relaxed thumb crossings
- a clear shape at the top and bottom

If one hand sounds blurrier, that hand needs to act less and listen more.
<!-- block -->
Now play both hands, but the shadow hand enters **one eighth note later**. Keep the same fingering and the same dynamic in both hands.

This tiny delay should feel awkward at first. That is good. You are teaching both hands to stay organized without hiding inside perfect unison.
<!-- block -->
Next comes the **light-and-dark round**. The leading hand plays **mezzo-forte**, while the shadow hand plays **piano**. After three clean reps, swap the roles.

If the soft hand gets sloppy or the loud hand gets harsh, lower the tempo and keep the contrast musical.
<!-- block -->
Boss round: build a **4-pass routine**:
1. right hand leads
2. left hand leads
3. right hand loud / left hand soft
4. left hand loud / right hand soft

Do the whole routine without stopping. Your goal is not speed. Your goal is clear hierarchy with steady rhythm.
<!-- block -->
Ask yourself:
- Which hand loses shape when it becomes the shadow?
- Did the delayed entry improve my listening?
- Could I keep both hands expressive without rushing?

Final upgrade: move the whole shadow play to **G major / E minor**. If both hands still sound connected, your scales just became music. 🎹
