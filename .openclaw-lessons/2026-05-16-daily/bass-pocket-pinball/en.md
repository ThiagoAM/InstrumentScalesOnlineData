---
name: Bass Pocket Pinball
description: Bounce between roots, ghost notes, and rebounds to make your major-scale groove feel alive and controlled.
---
Choose **G major** or **D major** and set a metronome to **90-106 BPM**. Picture your bass line as a pinball table: roots are the bumpers, ghost notes are the soft ricochets, and every strong landing should send the groove somewhere useful instead of somewhere noisy. The goal is not random motion — it is controlled bounce.
<!-- block -->
Round 1 is the **bumper round**. Build a one-bar groove using root, fifth, and one passing note from the scale. Play it repeatedly until beat 1 lands like a bumper every single time. If the line starts to rush, remove notes before you raise the tempo.
<!-- block -->
Round 2 is the **ricochet round**. Keep the same groove shape, but change one detail per bar:
- replace a note with a ghost note
- add a short eighth-note rest
- approach the root from above
- approach the root from below
- jump to the octave, then return immediately

Your time feel should stay heavy even when the path gets bouncy.
<!-- block -->
Round 3 is the **multiball round**. Play four bars where bars 1 and 3 stay simple, but bars 2 and 4 get busier. The trick is making the busy bars feel like an exciting rebound, not like the whole machine broke. Strong players know how to add motion without losing authority.
<!-- block -->
Final challenge: create **two four-bar grooves** in the same key. One should feel dry and stubborn; the other should feel springy and playful. Both must include a ghost note, one rest, and one clear return to the root on a strong beat. Then ask:
- Which version kept the deepest pocket?
- Did the ghost note add direction or just decoration?
- Could I bounce around without sounding unfocused?

When a bass line really grooves, every rebound tells the listener, “Relax — I know exactly where beat 1 lives.” 🎸
